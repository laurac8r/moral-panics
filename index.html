It's Never New

The recurring shape of moral panics. The same machine that mocked each generation's books, music, and games has, far more often, been pointed at people — and that version doesn't just fade. It writes laws, draws borders, and fills graves.

Content warning. This page documents real historical persecution and violence, described plainly — including genocide and the Holocaust, slavery's aftermath, lynching and racial terror, the incarceration and expulsion of whole communities, the persecution and criminalization of LGBTQIA2+ people, the abuse of Indigenous children, antisemitic blood libel, sexual violence, suicide, and the AIDS epidemic. Several of these panics are still ongoing. Please take care of yourself while reading.

“Every generation thinks their moral panic is brand new. It never is.” — Jessica Kellgren-Fozard, Why Anti-Trans Arguments Feel So Familiar

One machine, two kinds of target

Strip away the specifics and almost every panic runs the same play: a manufactured threat — usually to children, purity, or “our way of life” — is pinned on something different, alarm is raised by authorities, and the public is told the danger is unprecedented. The target comes in two shapes.

A new thing

Often it's a medium the young love and adults don't get — a book, a beat, a screen. These scares mostly burn out and fade into embarrassment. The timeline below.

A kind of person

Far more often, the same machine is aimed at a group of people. Those panics don't simply pass — they build prisons, closets, and camps. The two timelines after it.

When the panic is about a thing

Pick almost any era and you find adults convinced a new thing is destroying the young. The costume changes; the plot doesn't.

  1. c. 370 BCE · WritingThe written word

    In Plato's Phaedrus, Socrates warns that writing things down will hollow out memory and breed only the appearance of wisdom.

    “This invention will produce forgetfulness in the souls of those who learn it.”

  2. 1700s · The novel“Reading madness”

    Physicians and moralists warned that novels would inflame young women's passions, ruin them for real life, and breed an addictive escape from reality.

  3. 1800s · The waltzDancing too close

    The waltz — couples touching, turning face to face — was denounced as obscene and a direct threat to female virtue and public morals.

  4. 1954 · Comic booksSeduction of the Innocent

    Psychiatrist Fredric Wertham's book blamed comic books for juvenile delinquency, triggering U.S. Senate hearings and an industry censorship code.

  5. 1950s · Rock 'n' rollThe devil's music

    Elvis was filmed only from the waist up on national TV; preachers and parents called the music a corrupting influence luring the young toward ruin.

  6. 1980s · Dungeons & DragonsThe Satanic Panic

    A tabletop game was cast as a gateway to the occult, suicide, and murder — part of a wider wave of fear about hidden satanic influence on children.

  7. 1993–2005 · Video gamesTrains killers

    Mortal Kombat and Grand Theft Auto drew Senate hearings and a new rating board; games were blamed for real-world youth violence.

  8. 2010s–now · SmartphonesThe anxious generation

    Phones and social media are blamed for an attention-wrecked, anxious generation — the same fear about the young and a new medium, lit by a different screen.

When the panic is about a person — LGBTQIA2+ lives

The histories below are documented persecutions, not media scares. They sit on this page not because they equal a comic-book panic in gravity — they vastly exceed it — but because they were built with the same tool: a manufactured threat to children or purity, aimed at people for being different. Recognizing the pattern is how it gets resisted.

  1. 1513–1990s · Colonial AmericasThe “sodomite savage”

    Conquistadors and missionaries cast Indigenous third-gender and Two-Spirit roles as “sodomy” and savagery to justify conquest — Balboa's men killed roughly 40 Cueva people by war-dog in 1513. Later, U.S. and Canadian boarding and residential schools (1879–1996, “Kill the Indian, save the man”) enforced a rigid gender binary on Native children.

    CostExecutions, forced conversion, and the near-erasure of honored gender roles — only now being reclaimed (“Two-Spirit” was coined in 1990).

  2. 1885–1967 · United KingdomThe Labouchère Amendment & Oscar Wilde

    An 1885 law criminalized “gross indecency” between men, sold as a defense of public morality. Its vagueness made it a “blackmailer's charter.”

    CostOscar Wilde was sentenced to two years' hard labour in 1895; the same offence convicted Alan Turing in 1952. Not repealed in England & Wales until 1967.

  3. 1871–1945 · GermanyParagraph 175 & the pink triangle

    On the books from 1871 and broadened by the Nazis in 1935, homosexuality was framed as a contagion sapping the German Volk — to be “cleansed” from the body politic.

    Cost~100,000 men arrested; 5,000–15,000 sent to concentration camps, marked with the pink triangle, with very high death rates. The law survived into West Germany until 1994.

  4. 1950s · United StatesThe Lavender Scare

    Alongside the Red Scare, gay federal workers were branded “security risks” — supposedly perverts open to Soviet blackmail. Eisenhower's Executive Order 10450 (1953) barred them from government work.

    CostThousands fired or denied jobs; ruined careers, forced outings, and suicides. The State Department apologized in 2017.

  5. 1977–1988 · US & UK“Save Our Children” → Section 28

    Anita Bryant's 1977 campaign argued gay people “must recruit” children; California's Briggs Initiative (1978) tried to fire gay teachers; Britain's Section 28 (1988–2003) banned schools from “promoting” homosexuality.

    CostCivil-rights repeals and copycat campaigns; 15 years of legally silencing support for LGBT students in British schools.

  6. 1981–1989 · United StatesThe AIDS “gay plague”

    AIDS was cast as deserved punishment — a “gay plague” and divine judgment. Prominent voices floated quarantining and tattooing HIV-positive people.

    CostYears of government inaction accelerated mass death — roughly 70,000 Americans dead by the end of 1989 — while stigma drove people away from testing and care.

  7. 2016–present · United States“Bathroom bills” & the “groomer” panic

    Trans people were recast as bathroom predators (North Carolina's HB2, 2016) and LGBTQ people and drag performers as “groomers” indoctrinating children — the same recruitment lie, a new target.

    CostA record wave of restrictive bills (~510 anti-LGBTQ bills tracked in 2023), armed intimidation at Pride and drag events, and “groomer” normalized as a slur.

When the panic is about a person — racial, ethnic & religious minorities

The same four beats — manufactured threat, folk devil, alarm, law and violence — drove some of history's worst atrocities. They are gathered here to make the recurring machinery legible, not to flatten the differences between them.

  1. 12th c. onward · EuropeThe blood libel

    Jews were falsely accused of ritually murdering Christian children for their blood — the death of any child near Easter could be blamed on the local Jewish community as a hidden, organized crime.

    CostCenturies of pogroms, torture-extracted “confessions,” and expulsions (England expelled its Jews in 1290). Condemned as false by popes from 1247, yet it recurred into the 20th century (the Beilis trial, 1911–13).

  2. c. 1450–1750 · Europe & New EnglandThe witch trials

    Sold as a defensive war against a satanic conspiracy hidden among neighbors — a “deviant in our midst,” overwhelmingly women, blamed for plague, crop failure, and misfortune.

    CostTens of thousands executed across Europe; in Salem (1692) nineteen were hanged and one pressed to death on “spectral evidence.” Now a paradigm of gendered persecution.

  3. 1830s–1850s · United StatesAnti-Catholic nativism

    Irish Catholic immigrants were cast as agents of a “popish plot” loyal to the Pope; lurid convent-atrocity hoaxes (Maria Monk, 1836) framed Catholic institutions as threats to women and the Republic.

    CostMob arson against convents and churches, sustained discrimination, and the nativist Know-Nothing party briefly capturing statehouses.

  4. 1870s–1882 · United States“Yellow Peril” & Chinese Exclusion

    “The Chinese Must Go!” — Chinese workers were cast as an unassimilable, disease-carrying horde degrading white labor and bloodlines.

    CostThe Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) — the first U.S. law barring a nationality — six decades of exclusion, and mob massacres like Rock Springs (1885, at least 28 killed).

  5. 1880s–1955 · United StatesThe “Black beast rapist” myth

    After emancipation, Black men were cast as innate predators of white women — a fabricated threat used to frame lynching as “chivalry” and segregation as protection.

    CostThousands of spectacle lynchings and the legal architecture of Jim Crow. The Birth of a Nation (1915) revived the Klan; the 1955 murder of Emmett Till and his killers' acquittal helped ignite the civil rights movement.

  6. 1903–1945 · Russia → Nazi GermanyThe Protocols & the Holocaust

    The forged Protocols of the Elders of Zion (1903) packaged Jews as a hidden world conspiracy steering governments and economies. Nazi propaganda fused it with pseudo-scientific “racial hygiene” into an existential threat.

    CostThe laws, ghettos, and camps that murdered roughly six million Jews — the word “genocide” was coined (1944) to name it.

  7. 1930s · United StatesReefer Madness

    Cannabis was sold as a foreign “killer weed” driving Mexican laborers and Black musicians to violence and corrupting white youth — Harry Anslinger's explicitly racialized campaign.

    CostFederal prohibition (Marihuana Tax Act, 1937) enforced disproportionately on Black and Latino communities for generations.

  8. 1942 · United StatesJapanese American incarceration

    After Pearl Harbor, “Yellow Peril” fear was redirected at Japanese Americans, framed as a “fifth column” of spies loyal to the enemy.

    CostExecutive Order 9066 imprisoned ~120,000 people without charge on ancestry alone — no espionage was ever proven. Acknowledged as injustice with a 1988 apology and reparations.

  9. 1980s–1990s · United States“Crack babies” & “superpredators”

    Officials and media warned of brain-damaged “crack babies” and coming “superpredators” — implicitly Black, remorseless youth criminals — casting Black youth as an irreparable menace.

    CostThe 100-to-1 crack-sentencing disparity and a wave of laws trying children as adults — now recognized as engines of racially skewed mass incarceration.

  10. 2001–2010s · United StatesThe post-9/11 anti-Muslim panic

    Ordinary Muslims were recast as a hidden “enemy within” whose faith and “sharia law” supposedly menaced the nation.

    CostSurging hate crimes; the NSEERS program registered ~80,000 men and placed ~13,000 in removal proceedings (none convicted of terrorism); blanket surveillance and anti-sharia laws later struck down as unconstitutional.

The recurring template

Every panic above — about a thing or a person — runs the same four beats. Recognizing the script is how you tell a real harm from a recycled fear.

Something is singled out

A new medium, or a group of people — usually one already seen as different, foreign, or hard to defend.

“Think of the children”

The threat is framed as corruption of youth, innocence, purity, or “our way of life” — a danger no decent person could ignore.

Authorities sound the alarm

Experts, clergy, press, and hearings lend the fear the weight of certainty — and insist it has never been this bad before.

It hardens — or it fades

Aimed at a thing, the panic usually fades into embarrassment. Aimed at people, it leaves laws, segregation, and graves — and some of these are still running.
